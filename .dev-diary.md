## The idea Phase Part 2
 Ignoring Claude's top pick, I went with **Steelman**. I sent the idea to **Google Gemini** for a changeup.

**Prompt:**

Idea for a simple app: Argument Steelman-er You paste in any opinion or hot take, and the app generates the strongest possible case for the opposing side — not a strawman, a steelman. Perfect for debate prep, critical thinking, or escaping echo chambers

**Response:**

This is a compelling and timely idea. While "Devil's Advocate" tools exist, a dedicated Steelman-er app fills a unique gap: it doesn't just argue for the sake of being contrary; it helps users find the "grain of truth" in views they find repulsive or wrong.
Here is a blueprint for how you could build this, from the psychological framework to the technical execution.
1. The Core Logic: "Rapoport’s Rules"
The "secret sauce" of a true steelman is a set of rules popularized by philosopher Daniel Dennett (borrowed from Anatol Rapoport). Your app’s AI prompt should be hard-coded to follow these four steps:
Re-express the position: Summarize the opposing view so clearly and fairly that the opponent says, "Thanks, I wish I’d thought of putting it that way."
List points of agreement: Identify what both sides actually agree on (e.g., "We both want a safer society").
State what you learned: Mention anything you’ve learned from the opponent’s view.
The Steelman: Only then, construct the strongest possible version of their argument using the best available data, even if the user didn't provide it.
2. Feature Set
The "Heat" Slider: Let users set how "hot" the take is. A low setting gives a calm, academic steelman; a high setting mimics a passionate, persuasive speech from a true believer.
The ITT (Ideological Turing Test) Mode: A game mode where the app gives you a topic, and you have to write the steelman. The AI then grades you on whether you sound like a genuine advocate or if your bias "leaked" through.
Source Injection: The app shouldn't just generate text; it should pull in 1–2 real-world citations (via Perplexity or Google Search APIs) that support the opposing side’s strongest claims.
The "Weak Point" Highlighter: After generating the steelman, it points to the specific part of the user's original take that is most vulnerable to this new, stronger argument.
3. Potential Tech Stack
You can build this as a "wrapper" app relatively quickly:
Frontend: Flutter or React Native (for a "simple" cross-platform mobile feel).
Reasoning Engine: GPT-4o or Claude 3.5 Sonnet. These models are exceptionally good at "persona" adoption and logical reconstruction.
System Prompting: You would use a hidden system prompt like: "You are a world-class debater. Your goal is to pass the Ideological Turing Test for the opposing view of the following statement. Do not use strawmen, do not be condescending, and use the most charitable interpretation of their values."
4. Use Cases & "The Why"
Debate & Essay Prep: Students use it to anticipate the strongest moves their opponent will make.
Journalism: Reporters use it to ensure they aren't accidentally misrepresenting a community or viewpoint they aren't part of.[1][2]
Corporate Strategy: Leadership teams use it to "red team" a new product idea (e.g., "Give me the strongest reason why our customers will hate this subscription model").
The "Echo Chamber" Escape: A simple tool for people who feel stuck in a social media bubble and want to remember what the "other side" actually thinks (vs. what their feed says they think).
5. Why it's better than a standard Chatbot
If you ask a standard AI "Give me the other side," it often gives a "both sides" balanced summary that feels wishy-washy. A dedicated Steelman-er is more aggressive. It temporarily "takes a side" to help the user grow, making it a tool for Intellectual Empathy rather than just an information bot.
